Social Problem

In America, women’s incarceration rates have increased exponentially over the past couple of decades. As women are held in prisons and jails, it is their families especially the children who suffer. According to federal and state demographic surveys, three-fourths of female prisoners are prisoners, two-thirds with dependents under eighteen years of age. Multitudes of issues stand between incarcerated women and their children resulting in behavioral challenges and compound emotional issues. With such a high prevalence of imprisoned mothers, legislation needs to be put into place to protect families. Many mothers struggling with intersecting issues such as poverty, homelessness, lack of economic opportunity, transportation barriers, etc. also have histories of physical and sexual trauma, and substance abuse. These mothers then become entangled with legal systems and child welfare systems, and family preservation falls to the wayside. For many single mothers, entering the legal system means being forced to relinquish all parental rights as programs are not in place to support reunification or family preservation.

Four to nine percent of women enter prison while pregnant. Historically, it has been all too common for pregnant women to not have access to proper medical care while in prison. Adequate healthcare is mandated to all inmates through the Eighth Amendment to the Constitution, however, women still are provided scarcer healthcare and rehabilitative programs than men. As a result, many incarcerated women face unnecessarily high-risk pregnancies. On top of that, the practice of shackling women during childbirth including labor, delivery, and postpartum has been a norm that increases the risk of complications. This practice is not only inhumane but poses a problem for healthcare providers in cases of emergency. After minimal education surrounding pregnancy, incarcerated females are likely to have minimal if any post-birth bonding with their child. Many infants are taken away within the first twenty-four hours following birth and handed over to kin or more commonly the foster care system. From the start, these women are not given the opportunity to raise above their circumstance. They then become mothers without opportunity to connect and foster a supportive relationship with their child.

Women report high rates of physical and sexual victimization during incarceration. Only a handful of correctional settings have specially trained staff to address the unique and pervasive needs of female inmates. The rapid growth of women’s incarceration means that recent criminal justice reforms have not kept up with the number and needs of incarcerated women. America’s prisons have a long way to go to consider the needs of women, their families, and how to support long-term goals of successful reintegration into society.
